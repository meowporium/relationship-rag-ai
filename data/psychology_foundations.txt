TOPIC: Attachment Styles Overview
Attachment theory explains how early relationships with caregivers shape how we connect with romantic partners. There are four main styles: Secure (comfortable with intimacy, trusts others), Anxious (craves closeness, fears abandonment), Avoidant (values independence, uncomfortable with closeness), and Fearful-Avoidant (wants closeness but fears it). Knowing your attachment style helps you understand your relationship patterns. Attachment styles can change with awareness and therapy.

TOPIC: The Four Horsemen by John Gottman
Psychologist John Gottman identified four patterns that predict relationship failure: (1) Criticism — attacking your partner's character vs. expressing a complaint. (2) Contempt — disrespect, eye-rolling, mockery. The most destructive of the four. (3) Defensiveness — making excuses instead of taking responsibility. (4) Stonewalling — shutting down and going silent. Antidotes: Use "I" statements, build appreciation habits, take responsibility, and take breaks when emotionally flooded.

TOPIC: Love Languages
Gary Chapman's Five Love Languages describe how people give and receive love: (1) Words of Affirmation — verbal compliments and appreciation. (2) Acts of Service — doing helpful things. (3) Receiving Gifts — thoughtful presents as symbols of love. (4) Quality Time — undivided attention and shared experiences. (5) Physical Touch — hugs, holding hands. Mismatched love languages cause disconnection even when both partners care deeply.

TOPIC: Healthy vs Unhealthy Relationship Signs
Healthy relationships include mutual respect, open communication, trust, and feeling emotionally safe. Unhealthy relationships include control, jealousy used as manipulation, isolation from support networks, and lack of accountability. A useful test: Do you feel more like yourself or less like yourself in this relationship? Healthy relationships expand who you are.

TOPIC: Codependency
Codependency is an excessive emotional reliance on a partner, often losing your own identity. Signs: you feel responsible for your partner's emotions, you cannot say no, your mood depends entirely on theirs. Codependency often develops from growing up in homes with addiction or emotional neglect. Healing involves learning to self-soothe, setting boundaries, and individual therapy.

TOPIC: Conflict Resolution Strategies
Healthy conflict is inevitable and even healthy. Principles: (1) Attack the problem, not the person. (2) Take breaks when flooded — it takes 20 minutes for the nervous system to calm. (3) Use repair attempts to de-escalate tension. (4) Accept influence — be willing to be changed by your partner's perspective. (5) Seek to understand before being understood.

TOPIC: Self-Esteem and Relationships
Low self-esteem shows up as needing constant validation, jealousy, and staying in toxic relationships out of belief that you don't deserve better. Building self-esteem: (1) Practice self-compassion. (2) Challenge negative self-talk. (3) Build competence through goals. (4) Set and maintain boundaries. A relationship cannot fill the gap left by low self-esteem — that work must be internal.

TOPIC: Healing From Toxic Relationships
Recovery from toxic relationships is a process. Stages include shock, bargaining, anger, grief, and acceptance. Common challenges: missing the person even though they hurt you (trauma bonding) and self-blame. Strategies: (1) No contact when possible. (2) Trauma-informed therapy. (3) Rebuild your support network. (4) Practice radical self-compassion — leaving was brave, not weak.
