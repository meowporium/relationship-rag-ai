TOPIC: Questions Worth Asking Before Staying
Before deciding to work on a relationship, it helps to be honest with yourself about a few things. Are you staying because you genuinely see a future, or because you're afraid of being alone? Are you in love with this person, or in love with the history and comfort you've built? Do you feel like yourself around them, or a smaller version of yourself? These aren't easy questions but they're important ones. Choosing to work on a relationship should come from genuine desire, not fear.

TOPIC: When Effort Is One-Sided
A relationship where only one person is putting in the effort is not a partnership — it's a caretaking arrangement. If you're the one always initiating conversations, always apologizing first, always making plans, always checking in, ask yourself: what would this relationship look like if you stopped doing all of that? Sometimes the answer tells you everything. A relationship requires two people who both want it to work. One person cannot carry it alone indefinitely without burning out.

TOPIC: Compatibility Is Not Something You Can Force
Sometimes two people genuinely care about each other but are fundamentally incompatible. Different life goals, different values, different ideas about what a relationship should look like. Love is not always enough to bridge those gaps. Wanting kids when your partner doesn't. Wanting to settle in one city when your partner craves movement. Needing emotional closeness when your partner is avoidant. These are not problems that communication alone will solve. Compatibility matters.

TOPIC: The Sunk Cost Trap In Relationships
The longer you've been with someone, the harder it becomes to leave — even if things aren't working. You've shared so much history, made so many plans, maybe moved in together or intertwined your lives. This is called the sunk cost trap. The time you've already spent is gone whether you stay or go. The only question worth asking is: does this relationship make my life better going forward? Years invested does not mean more years should be invested if the answer is no.

TOPIC: Patterns That Don't Change
Pay attention to patterns, not promises. Anyone can promise to change after a fight. The question is whether the behavior actually changes over weeks and months. If someone has shown you a pattern — of dismissiveness, of jealousy, of dishonesty, of emotional unavailability — and that pattern has repeated itself multiple times despite conversations and promises, you have real information about who that person is right now. People can change, but it requires genuine sustained effort, not just words after conflict.

TOPIC: When A Relationship Drains More Than It Gives
A relationship should, on balance, add to your life. It won't always be easy, and every relationship has hard seasons. But if you consistently feel more anxious, more exhausted, more confused, or more unhappy because of this relationship than you would be without it, that is data worth taking seriously. Love should not feel like a constant uphill battle. It shouldn't feel like something you have to survive. If being in this relationship is costing you your peace, your confidence, or your sense of self, that cost is real.

TOPIC: Red Flags People Explain Away
Red flags people commonly explain away: a partner who puts you down in front of others but calls it joking. A partner who controls what you wear, who you see, or where you go but calls it love or concern. A partner who goes silent for days as punishment after disagreements. A partner who tells you that you're too sensitive whenever you raise a concern. A partner who compares you unfavorably to others. None of these behaviors become acceptable because someone says they love you. Love is not just a feeling — it is also a set of behaviors.

TOPIC: Leaving Is Not Failure
There is a widespread idea that a relationship ending means it failed. This is not true. Some relationships are meaningful, real, and important even if they don't last forever. People grow, circumstances change, incompatibilities emerge over time. Choosing to leave a relationship that isn't working is not giving up — it can be one of the most honest and self-respecting decisions a person makes. Staying in something broken just to say you stayed is not strength. Knowing when something has run its course is wisdom.

TOPIC: What Staying For The Wrong Reasons Looks Like
Common wrong reasons to stay in a relationship: fear of being alone, not wanting to hurt the other person, worrying about what others will think, financial dependency, shared living arrangements, or simply the comfort of routine. None of these are reasons to stay in a relationship that isn't working. They are reasons why leaving feels hard — which is different. It's okay to acknowledge that leaving would be difficult while also acknowledging that staying might not be right.

TOPIC: Honest Check-In Questions
Some honest questions to sit with: Do you feel respected by your partner on a normal day, not just after an argument? Do you feel comfortable being yourself around them, or do you edit yourself? When you imagine your future, do you feel excited or tired? Do you trust them? Do they make you feel good about yourself more often than they make you feel bad? If a close friend described your relationship to you from the outside, what would you think? Sometimes we need to step outside our own feelings to see clearly.
