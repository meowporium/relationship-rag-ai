TOPIC: Situationships and the Talking Stage
A situationship is when two people act like a couple without ever defining what they are. It has all the emotional weight of a relationship with none of the commitment or clarity. These arrangements almost always benefit the person who wants less. If you've been "talking" to someone for months and still don't know where you stand, that is information. Someone who wants to be with you will make it clear. Ambiguity is not romantic mystery — it's usually just someone keeping their options open while enjoying your attention.

TOPIC: When To Ask For a Label
If you want a relationship and the other person keeps dodging the conversation or saying they "don't like labels," ask yourself why labels feel threatening to them. Labels are just clarity. They mean you're choosing each other. If someone genuinely wants you, being called your partner shouldn't be scary — it should feel obvious. The discomfort with labeling is often discomfort with committing, and that's worth knowing before you invest more of yourself.

TOPIC: Getting Strung Along in a Situationship
One of the most painful parts of a situationship is the hope. Every good moment feels like it might be the turning point. Every text, every night together feels like maybe this is becoming real. That cycle of hope and disappointment is exhausting and it keeps you stuck. At some point you have to ask: is this person moving toward me, or am I just convenient? If the answer is the latter, staying is costing you time you could spend on someone who actually wants what you want.

TOPIC: Long Distance Relationships
Long distance relationships work for some people and not for others. What separates the ones that survive is not love — it's logistics and a shared timeline. If there is no plan for when the distance ends, you're not really in a relationship with a future, you're in a relationship with hope. Hope alone won't sustain a relationship. Before committing hard to long distance, it's worth having an honest conversation about whether there is a real path to being together, and what that path actually looks like.

TOPIC: When Long Distance Is Slowly Killing the Relationship
Long distance amplifies everything. When things are good, the distance hurts because you want to be together. When things are bad, the distance makes it harder to resolve conflict, build intimacy, or feel secure. If you find yourself more anxious, more jealous, and more unhappy since going long distance, pay attention to that. Some relationships can survive distance. Others were only working because of proximity, and the distance is just revealing that. Be honest about which one yours is.

TOPIC: Jealousy and Trust in Long Distance
Jealousy is common in long distance relationships and it makes sense — you can't see what your partner's daily life looks like, who they're spending time with, or how they're feeling. But jealousy that turns into constant checking, demands for updates, or accusations is corrosive. Trust is not something you can force or surveil your way into. Either you trust this person or you don't. If you don't, either work on why or be honest that the distance is too much for you right now.

TOPIC: Family Pressure on Relationships in Indian and South Asian Contexts
In many South Asian families, relationships are not just between two people — they involve families, communities, expectations around caste, religion, career, and timing. The pressure to be with someone your family approves of, or to marry by a certain age, is real and heavy. This doesn't make your personal feelings invalid. Wanting someone your family doesn't approve of is not a character flaw. But navigating this is genuinely complicated and it's okay to acknowledge that without pretending it's simple.

TOPIC: When Family Disapproves of Your Partner
Family disapproval of a partner is one of the hardest relationship situations to navigate. Sometimes family members see things you don't — red flags, incompatibilities, patterns. Other times they're reacting to prejudice, tradition, or their own fears. The question worth sitting with is: are they concerned about something real about this person's character, or are they concerned about something that has nothing to do with whether this person is good to you? Those are very different situations that call for very different responses.

TOPIC: Choosing Between Family and a Partner
If you're at the point where you feel like you have to choose between your family and your partner, that's an enormous amount of pressure to carry. There's no universal right answer. What matters is that you make the choice based on clear thinking, not guilt or fear alone. Think about who this person is to you, what your life looks like with and without them, and whether the family's concerns are about your wellbeing or about their expectations. You're allowed to choose yourself and what makes your life meaningful.

TOPIC: Self-Love Is Not a Buzzword
Self-love gets talked about a lot and it's become a bit of a cliche. But at its core it just means treating yourself with the same basic respect you'd offer a good friend. It means not staying in situations that hurt you just because leaving is hard. It means noticing when you're making yourself smaller for someone else. It means being honest about what you need instead of just adapting to what everyone else wants. You can't pour from an empty cup — that's not a motivational poster, it's just true.

TOPIC: Being Single Is Not a Problem to Solve
There's a lot of cultural pressure — especially in South Asian contexts — that treats being single past a certain age as something that needs to be explained or fixed. It's not. Being single and building a life you're proud of is not a consolation prize. It's a valid choice. Rushing into a relationship because you're scared of being alone, or because of family pressure, or because your friends are all coupling up, is how people end up in relationships that make them miserable. Being alone is better than being lonely next to someone.

TOPIC: Healing After a Relationship Before Starting a New One
There's no fixed timeline for how long it takes to get over someone. But jumping immediately into a new relationship to avoid the pain of a breakup is a very common pattern that usually just delays the grief and sometimes drags another person into your mess. You don't have to be perfectly healed to date again, but it's worth asking whether you're ready to genuinely invest in someone new, or whether you're mostly looking for distraction. New people deserve more than being a rebound.

TOPIC: Knowing Your Own Patterns
One of the most valuable things you can do after a relationship ends is look honestly at your own patterns. Did you ignore red flags early on? Did you give too much too fast? Did you stay too long out of fear? Did you shut down when things got hard? Not to beat yourself up about it, but because patterns repeat until we understand them. The goal isn't to blame yourself — it's to understand yourself well enough that your next relationship starts from a more honest place.

TOPIC: What Self-Respect Actually Looks Like in a Relationship
Self-respect in a relationship looks like being able to say "that's not okay with me" and meaning it. It looks like not chasing someone who pulls away. It looks like being able to walk away from something that isn't working, even if you still have feelings for the person. It looks like knowing your own needs and not apologizing for having them. It's not about being cold or guarded — it's about having a baseline standard for how you expect to be treated and actually holding to it.
